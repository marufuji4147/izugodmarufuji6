Izu is not just a destination. It is one of the oldest sacred territories in Japan — a peninsula where volcanic earth, ancient shrines, and the deep Pacific have coexisted for millennia. The gods never left.

Why Izu is Different

Three Forces That Shape This Land

Izu's spiritual power comes from the meeting of three ancient forces. No other place in Japan holds all three in such proximity.

I Fire of the Earth 火

Volcanic Power

Izu sits on one of the most active volcanic zones on earth. At Hashiriyu — Japan’s rarest horizontal spring — boiling water erupts directly from a sea cliff into the Pacific. The earth breathes here. You can hear it.

II Breath of the Forest 木

Ancient Life

A camphor tree that has been alive for 2,100 years stands at Kinomiya Shrine. It was already 600 years old when the shrine was built around it. In Shinto belief, it is not a tree — it is a god (神木, shinboku).

III Voice of the Sea 海

The Eternal Ocean

Izu’s coastline is marked by shimenawa — sacred ropes tied between sea rocks to mark the presence of the divine. The fishermen of Ajiro Port have prayed at these same rocks for a thousand years. The sea gives, and the sea is sacred.

神の宿る地で、魚は聖なる糧となり、山は気を吐き、大地は湧く。 その山と海の恵みから生まれた魚を、丸藤は一本の節として仕上げる。

“In a land where gods dwell, fish become sacred, mountains breathe, and the earth runs hot. It is from these mountains and this sea that Marufuji draws one block of katsuobushi at a time.”

What is BUSHI MESHI

Japan's Oldest Soul Food, Perfected Here

BUSHI MESHI is the art of eating — a bowl of perfectly steamed rice blanketed with freshly hand-shaved katsuobushi (dried bonito flakes), a pour of golden dashi, and a touch of soy sauce. It sounds simple. That simplicity is the entire point.

At Marufuji (丸藤), based in Izu, Shizuoka, we have spent generations mastering the precision of the shave. At 0.015mm — thinner than a human hair — the flakes melt on hot rice like whispers of smoke, releasing centuries of umami in a single breath.

Sacred Collaboration

Where Dashi Meets the Divine

Atami is one of the most spiritually charged cities in Japan. Two of its ancient shrines — each with centuries of pilgrims, warlords, and answered prayers — now share a story with BUSHI MESHI and Marufuji.

Suggested Itinerary

One Perfect Day in Izu

We handle transport, translation, and timing — so you focus entirely on creating. Here's how a typical day unfolds.

⛩️

07:30 · First Light

Izusan Shrine — 837 Steps to the Sea

伊豆山神社 (Izusan Jinja) before the crowds. Climb the ancient stone steps through cedar forest as morning mist rises over Sagami Bay. Where Yoritomo prayed before founding the Kamakura shogunate. Your most powerful opening shot.

🌳

09:30 · Morning

Kinomiya Shrine — The 2,100-Year Tree

Walk the bamboo tunnel to Japan’s second-largest sacred camphor. Circle the 24-meter trunk, whisper your wish, feel something shift. Iconic content in daylight; unforgettable at night with the lanterns.

☀️

11:00 · Late Morning

Joren Falls

Drive into Izu to 浄蓮の滝 (Joren no Taki). Mist over the emerald pool, wasabi growing wild along the stream — pure, unrepeatable light for your nature content.

🌊

14:30 · Afternoon

Jogasaki Suspension Bridge — 城ケ崎つりはし

Walk out onto the 48-meter suspension bridge at 城ヶ崎海岸, swaying above a gorge carved by volcanic eruptions 4,000 years ago. Below, the Pacific hammers black lava cliffs with relentless force. Ahead, the Izu coastline stretches toward the open horizon. The bridge is one of the most-photographed spots in Shizuoka — a single frame that captures the raw, ancient power of this sacred land. Bring a camera. Your audience will feel the drop.

Return to 来宮神社 at dusk, when 160 lanterns ignite the ancient forest in amber. The great camphor glows. The most beautiful shot of the day — and we bring you here at the quietest hour.
